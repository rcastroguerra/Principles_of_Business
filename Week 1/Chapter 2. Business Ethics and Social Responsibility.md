    LO 2-1 Business Ethics and Social Responsibility
  </a>
</h1>

Every organization, including nonprofits, must manage the ethical behavior of employees and participants in its operations. Firms that maintain high ethical standards tend to be more profitable and have more satisfied employees and customers. Unethical behavior can harm profits, as seen in cases involving companies like Credit Suisse, Luckin Coffee, and Activision Blizzard. Most firms, however, strive to maintain an ethical culture to prevent misconduct.

This chapter examines the role of ethics and social responsibility in business decision-making. It defines business ethics, explains its importance, and explores common ethics issues so readers can recognize them in practice. The chapter also provides steps companies can take to improve ethical behavior. The second half focuses on social responsibility and unemployment, highlighting key issues and how companies respond to them.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Business ethics
</span></h2>

Business ethics refers to the principles and standards that determine acceptable conduct in organizations. Personal ethics relates to an individual's values and standards of behavior. Acceptable behavior in business is influenced not only by the organization but also by stakeholders, including employees, customers, competitors, government regulators, interest groups, and the public.

The importance of business ethics is highlighted by publicity and debates over legal and ethical issues at well-known firms. For example, Activision Blizzard faced lawsuits alleging sexual harassment and gender discrimination. Over 700 incidents were reported, and employees protested the company’s handling of these issues. One lawsuit cited the company’s culture and management as contributing factors.

Unethical activities often stem from an organizational culture that encourages bending the rules. Conversely, trust in business is crucial for maintaining relationships. According to surveys (see Figure 2.1), trust in industries like financial services is lower than in others, reflecting the impact of corporate misconduct and media coverage.

> Key takeaway: Integrating ethics and social responsibility into all business decisions is essential for maintaining trust, protecting the organization’s reputation, and ensuring long-term success.

Organizations with a strong ethical culture encourage employees to act with integrity and follow the company’s values. For example, Colgate-Palmolive requires all employees to complete a mandatory ethics course to unify staff under the organization’s guiding principles. Ethical leadership, clearly communicated values, and compliance programs are critical to fostering good business ethics.

To establish a true ethical culture, managers must demonstrate a “tone at the top”, which includes:

- Supporting ethics and compliance personally
- Clearly communicating ethical expectations to all employees
- Educating managers and supervisors about company policies
- Training employees on how to respond during an ethics crisis

Businesses should aim not only to make a profit but also to consider the ethical and social impact of their activities. For instance, Walmart and Sam’s Club have donated over 7 billion pounds of food to local Feeding America food banks. Profits enable companies to contribute to society, and firms with strong social contributions tend to be more profitable.

Social responsibility is defined as a business’s obligation to maximize positive impact and minimize negative impact on society. While closely related, ethics and social responsibility are not the same:

- Business ethics deals with right and wrong decisions of individuals or workgroups.
- Social responsibility concerns the broader impact of the company’s activities on society.

> Example: From an ethical perspective, overcharging Medicare is wrong. From a social responsibility perspective, the concern is how this affects access to healthcare for all citizens.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Omaze: Reinventing the Charity Model
</span></h2>

Omaze is an online global sweepstakes platform that raises money for charities by offering exclusive experiences and luxury prizes. Since its launch in 2012, Omaze, a for-profit company, has raised over $150 million for more than 600 charities.

In Omaze’s business model:

- The company typically keeps 20% of donations for operational costs, marketing, and profit.
- The remaining 80% goes toward the experience costs and the nonprofit.
- Campaigns are either celebrity-driven or prize-driven. Omaze initially focused heavily on celebrity experiences but shifted strategy after a $233,000 McLaren sweepstakes raised $1.9 million for the Movember Foundation. Now, they run fewer celebrity campaigns and focus on pairing high-demand items with causes that resonate with entrants.

Omaze’s marketing team highlights charities and collaborates with influencers to promote campaigns. By combining purpose-driven impact with business growth, Omaze demonstrates that companies can scale social impact and profits simultaneously.

Critical Thinking Highlights:

1. How does Omaze make money? 
   - Keeps ~20% of donations for operational costs and profit.
2. Strengths in partnerships: 
    - Strong marketing, celebrity connections, targeted campaigns, and focus on high-demand prizes.
3. Profit-driven, purpose-driven, or both? 
   - Both; Omaze balances profitability with social impact.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Business Ethics, Social Responsibility, and Business Law
</span></h2>

The most basic ethical and social responsibility concerns are often codified in laws and regulations that encourage businesses to follow society’s standards, values, and attitudes. These rules aim to institutionalize ethical conduct and prevent harm to customers, the environment, and other stakeholders.

Business law governs the conduct of business and helps owners, managers, and employees avoid problems or conflicts. Professionals in accounting, finance, and marketing must understand laws relevant to their work. For example, the Dodd-Frank Act was passed to reform the financial industry and protect consumers from complex or deceptive financial products.

Managers are expected to obey all laws and regulations, though not all unethical actions are illegal. Recent concerns, such as identity theft, show that companies also have an ethical responsibility to protect customer data.

Together, business ethics, social responsibility, and laws act as a compliance system, guiding businesses and employees to act responsibly in society. Ethics can serve as both:

- A buffer to prevent illegal conduct

- A positive force to build loyal customers and employees

> Legal and ethical concerns evolve over time, so businesses must stay aware of changing societal expectations.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Role of Ethics in Business
</span></4>


### Key Points on Business Ethics

#### 1. Public Awareness of Ethics
- Media like *The Wall Street Journal* and *USA Today* highlight growing concerns over legal and ethical issues in business.
- Example: **Nikki Pope** at NVIDIA emphasizes building **trustworthy and responsible AI**.
- Societal judgment of unethical behavior affects a company’s ability to meet its goals, regardless of personal opinions.

#### 2. Recognition for Ethical Conduct
- Firms like **3M** view ethics as a competitive advantage and consistently appear on the *World’s Most Ethical Companies* list.
- Public perception often exaggerates misconduct because positive ethical actions are less reported.

#### 3. Ethical Conflicts and Legal Issues
- Misconduct often begins as **ethical conflicts** but can escalate to legal disputes.
- **Ethical gray areas** occur in new, ambiguous, or unregulated situations, e.g., sharing economy platforms like Uber, Lyft, Airbnb.
- Examples of misconduct: exploitation of workers, safety issues, harassment, stealing trade secrets, regulatory violations.

#### 4. Ethics Beyond the Law
- Ethical conduct builds **trust** in relationships, essential for business success.
- Lack of trust (e.g., misleading employees or colleagues) damages relationships and organizational reputation.

#### 5. Ethics in All Sectors
- Ethical issues occur in **government, sports, nonprofits, and science**, not just business.
- Misconduct can harm relationships with **customers, suppliers, investors**, and employee retention.

#### 6. Learning and Judgment
- Individuals are judged by superiors, peers, and family for ethical behavior.
- Recognizing and resolving ethical issues is crucial for **sound decision-making in business**.

#### Purpose
- Detect and understand ethical issues that may arise in business.

#### What is an Ethical Issue?
- An **ethical issue** is a problem, situation, or opportunity requiring a choice among actions that may be **right or wrong, ethical or unethical**.
- Decision-making requires:
  - Good personal values
  - Knowledge and competence in the relevant business area
  - Awareness of organizational policies and codes of ethics
  - Consultation with coworkers or managers when needed
- Ethical dilemmas often have **gray areas**, e.g.:
  - Reporting a coworker engaging in time theft
  - Reporting a friend cheating on a test
  - Omitting product safety issues when presenting to a customer

#### Causes of Unethical Behavior
- Rewards for overly aggressive financial or business objectives

#### Categories of Ethical Issues
| Category | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Abusive or intimidating behavior | Workplace bullying or harassment |
| Conflicts of interest | Favoring friends or family in business decisions |
| Fairness and honesty | Manipulating information for personal gain |
| Communications | Hiding critical information from employees or customers |
| Misuse of company resources | Using company property for personal purposes |
| Business associations | Bribery, fraud, or unfair competition |

#### Observations from the Workplace
- Remote work (COVID-19) may reduce bullying and harassment but makes monitoring misconduct more difficult
- Companies must **adapt monitoring programs** to detect compliance violations
- Employees sometimes feel pressured to **compromise ethical standards**

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Bribery
</span></h2>

### Overview
- Many ethical issues in business appear simple but are actually **complex**.
- Understanding what is ethical often requires **years of business experience**.

### What Is Bribery?
- **Bribery** involves payments, gifts, or special favors intended to **influence a decision**.
- A bribe benefits an individual or company **at the expense of other stakeholders**.
- Bribery is considered **unethical and improper**.

### Legal Implications
- Bribery is **illegal in many countries**.
- In the **United States**, the **Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)** imposes severe penalties on companies that bribe foreign government officials.
- Companies operating internationally must be especially cautious.

### Cultural Differences
- Ethics are influenced by **culture**:
  - In the **United States**, giving an elaborate gift during a first business meeting may be seen as a bribe.
  - In **Japan**, not bringing a gift can be considered impolite.
- Understanding local culture is essential to determining what is ethical.
- Firms must follow **global business values and policies**.

### Gray Areas and Transparency
- Ethical gray areas exist with:
  - Business gifts
  - Meals
  - Entertainment
  - Financial incentives
- Example:
  - Pharmaceutical representatives may provide incentives to physicians but must **publicly report them** due to past conflicts of interest.
- Lack of **transparency** can create ethical issues.
- Research shows that providing meals can increase physicians’ prescribing of **brand-name drugs**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Misuse of Company Time (Time Theft)
</span></h2>



### Overview
- **Time theft** is a common form of workplace misconduct.
- It occurs when employees engage in activities **not necessary for their job** during work hours.

### Examples of Time Theft
- Late arrivals or leaving early
- Long lunch breaks
- Inappropriate use of sick days
- Excessive socializing
- Personal activities during work hours, such as:
  - Online shopping
  - Watching sports
  - Browsing social media

### Misuse of Company Resources
- Time theft often includes misuse of **company resources**, such as computers and internet access.
- Some companies block sites and apps like:
  - Facebook
  - YouTube
  - TikTok
  - Reddit

### Economic Impact
- Time theft is difficult to measure but is estimated to cost companies **hundreds of billions of dollars annually**.
- The average employee is believed to "steal" about **4.5 hours per week**.

### Notable Example
- On **Cyber Monday**, nearly **25% of employees** report shopping online while at work.

### Ethical Implications
- These behaviors result in **lost productivity and profits**.
- Time theft represents an ethical issue related to **honesty, fairness, and proper use of company resources**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Abusive and Intimidating Behavior
</span></h2>


### Overview
- **Abusive or intimidating behavior** is the most common ethical problem faced by employees.
- It ranges from minor distractions to serious workplace disruptions.
- Examples include:
  - Physical threats
  - False accusations
  - Profanity and insults
  - Yelling, harshness, or unreasonable behavior
  - Ignoring coworkers or persistent annoyance
- Perceptions vary: what one person sees as yelling, another may see as normal speech.

### Civility and Productivity
- Declining civility in society affects the workplace.
- Organizations lose productivity due to time spent resolving abusive relationships.

### Cultural and Communication Challenges
- Abusive behavior is difficult to assess due to:
  - Cultural differences
  - Language and lifestyle diversity
  - Differences in age and social norms
- Words or expressions normal in one culture may be offensive in another.
- **Intent matters**:
  - A comment meant as a compliment may still be perceived as abusive.
  - Tone and voice inflection play a significant role.

### Bullying in the Workplace
- **Bullying** involves targeting an individual or group through:
  - Threats
  - Harassment
  - Belittling
  - Verbal abuse
  - Excessive criticism
- Bullying contributes to a **hostile work environment**, a concept often linked to sexual harassment.

### Sexual Harassment and Awareness
- Sexual harassment gained public attention through the **#MeToo movement**.
- The **U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)** reported a spike in sexual harassment charges during the movement’s rise, followed by a decline.
- Sexual harassment has legal recourse; **bullying currently has limited legal protection**.

### Organizational Responses
- Bullying and harassment threaten employee **health, safety, and organizational culture**.
- Companies such as:
  - Express
  - The Cheesecake Factory
  - McKinsey
  - Nike
  - Chevron
  - Mars
  - Uber
- have introduced **ombuds programs** to provide confidential and independent complaint resolution.

### Impact and Persistence
- Workplace bullying is increasing in the **United States**.
- It can cause serious psychological and health-related harm.
- Bullying persists partly because bullies often **outrank their victims**, discouraging reporting.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Misuse of Company Resources
</span></h2>


### Overview
- Misuse of company resources includes **using employer assets for personal benefit**.
- Such abuse can lead to **termination** and, in some cases, **legal consequences**.
- The **Ethics Resource Center** identifies this as a **leading form of organizational misconduct**.

### Employee Internal Theft
- Employee theft represents a **major loss of resources**, especially for retailers.
- Common examples include:
  - Hiding company items in handbags, backpacks, or briefcases
  - Overcharging customers and keeping the extra money
  - Shipping personal items using the company’s account
  - Contract workers stealing materials or office equipment
  - Food service employees giving free food or drinks to friends or customers
  - Hair stylists keeping cash payments without reporting them

### Other Forms of Resource Misuse
- Submitting personal expenses on company expense reports
- Using company vehicles for personal purposes
- Stealing office supplies
- Misusing corporate funds for:
  - Personal travel
  - Entertainment
  - Charitable donations

### Real-World Example
- A former executive at **Acacia Research Corporation** allegedly misused corporate funds for personal expenses.

### Prevention and Control
- Companies need:
  - Effective **monitoring systems**
  - **Employee training** on ethical standards
- These measures help prevent theft and misuse of organizational resources.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Policies to Prevent Misuse of Company Resources
</span></h2>

### Overview
- Businesses must have **policies in place** to prevent the abuse of company resources.
- Misuse of resources is a widespread problem that can cost companies **millions of dollars** and **thousands of hours of productivity**.

### Real-World Example
- **Coca-Cola** has an official policy outlining **acceptable use of company resources**:
  - Company assets **should not be used for personal gain** or outside business purposes.
  - Company resources **should not be used for illegal or unethical activities**.
- Such policies are common in **large companies** to protect against financial and productivity losses.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Conflict of Interest
</span></h2>

### Overview
- A **conflict of interest** occurs when an individual must choose between advancing **personal interests** or the **interests of others**, such as the company or stakeholders.
- Example:
  - A corporate manager should make decisions to ensure the company is profitable for its shareholders.
  - If the manager makes decisions that benefit themselves personally (money, power) at the company’s expense, a **conflict of interest** exists.

### Preventing Conflicts
- Employees must **separate personal financial interests from business dealings**.
- Organizations can implement **conflicts of interest policies**.  
- Example:
  - The **Federal Housing Administration (FHA)** states that underwriters, appraisers, inspectors, and engineers cannot have multiple roles or compensation sources from a single FHA-insured transaction.

### Examples of Conflicts of Interest
- **Insider trading**: buying or selling stocks using non-public material information.
- **Bribery**: can be considered a conflict of interest and is more prevalent in some countries than others.

### Global Context
- **Transparency International** publishes the **Corruption Perceptions Index** to measure perceived corruption globally.
- Countries perceived as most corrupt:  
  - Yemen, Syria, Venezuela, South Sudan, Somalia
- The **United States** ranks 27th (not shown in the table).

### Legal Implications
- Severe conflicts of interest can lead to **legal repercussions**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Fairness and Honesty
</span></h2>


### Definition
- **Fairness** means treating all stakeholders (customers, employees, clients, competitors) **justly and impartially**.  
- **Honesty** means **truthfulness, transparency, and integrity** in all business dealings.  
- These values are central to business ethics and guide decision-making beyond mere legal compliance.

### Overview
- Businesspersons are expected to:
  - Follow all applicable laws and regulations.
  - Avoid knowingly harming stakeholders through **deception, misrepresentation, coercion, or discrimination**.
- Fairness and honesty also relate to **proper use of company resources**.

### Dishonesty
- Dishonesty involves **lack of integrity, nondisclosure, and lying**.
- Common examples:
  - Theft of office supplies (e.g., pencils, Post-it notes)
  - Stealing expensive items or equipment (e.g., computers, software)
- Employees must understand company policies on theft and ethical behavior.

### Fair Competition
- Fairness involves maintaining ethical **competition practices**:
  - Example: A former **General Electric** employee stole trade secrets to benefit business partners in China.  
  - Antitrust issues: The **U.S. Justice Department** filed charges against **Google** for alleged anticompetitive behavior in online advertising. Google holds ~90% of the global search engine market.

### Disclosure and Product Safety
- Companies must disclose potential harm caused by products:
  - Example: **Core Health & Fitness** paid a $6.5 million civil penalty to the **U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission** for failing to report serious injuries related to defective exercise equipment.
  - The company also implemented an improved consumer safety compliance program.

### High-Profile Dishonesty Cases
- **Houston Astros**: fined $5 million for using video surveillance to steal opposing teams’ signals.  
- **Operation Varsity Blues**: Philip Esformes bribed the University of Pennsylvania’s basketball coach to help his son gain admission; part of a nationwide college admissions scandal orchestrated by **William Rick Singer**, involving over 50 people.  

### Key Takeaways
- Fairness and honesty go beyond legal compliance; they require **ethical decision-making** that protects stakeholders, promotes trust, and ensures transparency.


<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Communications
</span></h2>

### Definition
- **Communications** in business refers to the way companies convey information to **consumers, investors, and other stakeholders**.  
- Ethical communications require **truthfulness, transparency, and accuracy**, avoiding misleading or deceptive messages.

### Overview
- False or misleading advertising and deceptive personal-selling tactics can:
  - Anger consumers
  - Harm a company's reputation
  - Lead to financial or legal consequences

### Real-World Examples
- **Nikola Motor Company**:  
  - A startup zero-emission truck company staged a video showing a truck in operation.  
  - The company later admitted the truck was rolled down a hill.  
  - Paid **$125 million** in SEC charges for misleading investors.
- **Cigarette labeling**:  
  - The FDA warned manufacturers against using terms like **“additive-free”** or **“natural”** without proper disclosure, to prevent misleading consumers.  
  - Labels must indicate that products are **not safer** than others despite these claims.  
  - Products with hazardous substances have **stricter labeling requirements** for consumer protection.

### Truthful Product Claims
- Claims such as **“natural”** are often contested or litigated.  
- FDA guidelines state that for a product to be labeled natural:
  - Ingredients must be **derived from nature**
  - Ingredients must have **minimal processing**  
- Companies sometimes use **qualified claims** like “all natural flavors” or “no artificial preservatives” to comply with regulations.

### Key Takeaways
- Ethical communications ensure **trust with consumers and stakeholders**.  
- Companies must provide **accurate, clear, and transparent information** about products, services, and corporate practices.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Business Relationships
</span></h2>

### Definition
- **Business relationships** refer to the interactions and conduct of businesspersons with **customers, suppliers, employees, and other stakeholders**.  
- Ethical business relationships require **honesty, fairness, respect, and responsibility**, while avoiding actions that pressure others into unethical behavior.

### Overview
- Ethical behavior in business includes:
  - Keeping **company secrets**
  - Meeting **obligations and responsibilities**
  - Avoiding **undue pressure** on others to act unethically
- Managers have a special responsibility due to their **authority**:
  - They influence employee behavior
  - Must create an environment that supports organizational objectives **without compromising employee rights**

### Ethical Risks and Pressures
- Organizational pressures may encourage unethical actions, such as:
  - Invading privacy
  - Stealing competitor secrets
  - Manipulation or dishonesty
- Lack of ethical guidance from managers creates opportunities for misconduct.

### Real-World Example
- **Wells Fargo**:
  - Created **3.5 million fake accounts**, harming customers’ credit ratings.  
  - The **Federal Reserve** restricted the bank’s growth until oversight and risk management improved.

### Plagiarism in Business
- **Plagiarism**: taking someone else's work or ideas and presenting them as your own without proper acknowledgment.  
- Examples:
  - Copying reports or business documents without attribution
  - Managers claiming credit for a subordinate’s ideas

### Key Takeaways
- Ethical business relationships build **trust, loyalty, and organizational integrity**.  
- Managers play a crucial role in guiding ethical behavior and preventing misconduct.  
- Plagiarism and misappropriation of ideas undermine both individual and organizational credibility.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Questions to Consider in Determining Whether an Action Is Ethical
</span></h2>


### Definition
- Making decisions about ethical issues involves **recognizing potential ethical dilemmas** and evaluating actions in terms of legality, company policies, industry norms, and personal values.  
- Managers and employees may overlook ethical issues due to **focus on immediate or personal concerns**, but open discussion helps identify and resolve these issues.

### Overview
- Ethical issues often go unnoticed or receive limited scrutiny.  
- Managers sometimes make **intuitive decisions** without realizing the ethical dimensions involved.  
- Discussing ethical questions openly **promotes trust and learning** in organizations.  

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Key Questions (Table 2.5)
</span></h2>

| Question |
|----------|
| Are there any potential legal restrictions or violations that could result from the action? |
| Does your company have a specific code of ethics or policy on the action? |

| Would this activity be accepted by your co-workers? Will your decision or action withstand open discussion with co-workers and managers and survive untarnished? |

### Key Takeaways
- Recognition of ethical issues is the **first step toward resolution**.  
- Open discussion and evaluation using these questions help ensure **ethical decision-making** and alignment with both organizational and personal values.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Three Factors That Influence Business Ethics
</span></h2>

<img src="./img/fer85914_0202.png" width="800"></img>

### Definition
- Improving ethical behavior involves creating an environment where employees make **ethical decisions consistently**.  
- Ethical decisions are influenced by:  
  1. **Individual moral standards and values**  
  2. **Influence of managers and co-workers**  
  3. **Opportunity to engage in misconduct**  



### Factors Influencing Ethical Behavior
- **Individual Standards and Values**: Personal ethics guide employees’ actions and responsibility.  
- **Managers’ and Co-Workers’ Influence**: Authority and example of peers and supervisors significantly affect workplace decisions.  
- **Opportunity**:  
  - Availability of rewards or absence of barriers may encourage unethical behavior.  
  - Rewards include **bonuses or pay raises**; penalties include **demotions or termination**.  

### Causes of Ethical Conflict
- Employees often face tension between **personal ethics** and **organizational obligations**.  
- Conflict increases when a company:  
  - Encourages unethical conduct  
  - Exerts pressure to break rules  
- Employees may rely on **peer behavior** to guide decisions if policies are unclear.

### Codes of Ethics
- **Professional codes of ethics** are formalized rules describing what the company expects from employees.  
- Characteristics:  
  - Provide **guidelines and principles**, not every detailed scenario  
  - Help employees meet **organizational objectives ethically**  
  - Include input from **executives, board members, legal staff, and employees** across departments  

### Key Takeaways
- Ethical behavior depends on the **interplay between personal values, social influence, and opportunity**.  
- Establishing **clear ethics policies and codes** helps reduce conflicts and guide employees toward responsible actions.  
- Organizations that prioritize ethics **build trust, prevent misconduct, and improve long-term performance**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Promoting Ethical Behavior Through Codes, Policies, and Training
</span></h2>

### How Codes and Policies Advance Ethical Behavior
- Codes of ethics, policies on ethics, and **ethics training programs**:
  - Prescribe acceptable and unacceptable activities  
  - Limit opportunities for misconduct by establishing **punishments for violations**  
  - Encourage the creation of an **ethical culture** within the organization  
- Compliance requirements help establish **uniform behavior** among employees.  
- Organizations with **written codes, ethics training, ethics officers, hotlines, and reporting systems** are more likely to have employees report observed misconduct.  

### Enforcement and Reporting
- Enforcement through **rewards and punishments** increases employee acceptance of ethical standards.  
- **Anonymous reporting** helps protect employees from retaliation.  
- **Whistleblowing**: Employees expose employer wrongdoing to outsiders (media, government regulators).  
- The **Dodd-Frank Act** includes a whistleblower bounty program:
  - Employees can receive **10–30% of monetary sanctions over $1 million** for reporting corporate misconduct.  
- Example: The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has awarded **more than $1 billion to whistleblowers**.

### Cultural and Integrity-Based Initiatives
- Organizations are shifting from purely legal ethics programs to **cultural- or integrity-based initiatives**.  
- Integrating ethics into **core organizational values** improves business performance.  
- Positive outcomes of ethical programs include:
  - Increased **trust**, efficiency, and effectiveness  
  - Protection of **company reputation and product image**  
  - Improved **profitability, hiring, employee satisfaction, and customer loyalty**  

### Consequences of Weak Ethical Culture
- Organizations without strong ethics programs may experience:
  - Fewer employees adhering to organizational values  
  - Lack of preparedness to handle key risks  
  - Lower reporting of suspected wrongdoing  
  - Higher levels of misconduct  

**Key Insight:** A strong ethical culture is the **greatest determinant of future organizational misconduct**.

Many companies now focus on **diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI)**:

Overall, businesses have a responsibility to **create safe, fair, and inclusive workplaces where employees can contribute and succeed**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Summary: Consumer Relations
</span></h2>


A key responsibility of businesses is their **relationship with consumers**. Customers expect businesses to provide **safe, reliable, and satisfying products** while respecting their **rights as consumers**.

The actions taken by individuals and groups to protect consumer rights are known as **consumerism**. Consumer advocates may:
- Write letters to companies  
- Lobby government agencies  
- Create public service announcements  
- Boycott companies they consider irresponsible  

### Consumer Bill of Rights

In 1962, John F. Kennedy introduced the **Consumer Bill of Rights**, which identified four basic consumer rights:

1. **Right to Safety**  
   Businesses must not sell products that could cause harm or injury. Companies must also provide safe environments for customers when they shop.

2. **Right to Be Informed**  
   Consumers must receive **complete and accurate information** about products, including instructions, risks, and warnings on labels and packaging.

3. **Right to Choose**  
   Consumers should have access to **a variety of products and services at competitive prices**, along with good quality and fair service.

4. **Right to Be Heard**  
   Consumers have the right to **voice complaints and concerns**, and businesses and governments should consider these concerns when making policies.

Overall, businesses must ensure that they **protect consumer rights, provide safe products, and respond fairly to customer concerns**.


<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Case Summary: Sticky Fingers – Inside the Shoplifting Surge
</span></h2>

Shoplifting is a **multibillion-dollar problem** that has been increasing and significantly affecting retailers such as Target, Walgreens, Home Depot, Kroger, Best Buy, CVS, and Walmart. Retail theft reduces company earnings and impacts not only businesses but also **employees and consumers**.

Some theft is committed by individuals, while a large portion is linked to **organized retail crime**. In severe cases, stores have been forced to close locations where losses became too high to sustain operations.

One explanation for shoplifting is **ethical egoism**, a philosophy stating that people act mainly in their **own self-interest**. If individuals believe the **risk of getting caught is low** and the **benefits are high**, they may be more likely to steal. Others justify shoplifting because they believe large corporations are already highly profitable.

Retail businesses typically follow a **stakeholder approach**, meaning they consider the interests of multiple groups such as:
- Shareholders  
- Employees  
- Customers  
- Suppliers  
- Local communities  

When shoplifting increases and stores close, the effects spread across these stakeholders. Employees may lose their jobs, customers lose access to stores, communities lose tax revenue, suppliers lose business, and shareholders see reduced profits.

To reduce shoplifting, retailers use several prevention strategies:
- Placing vulnerable items deeper in the store  
- Locking expensive merchandise in secure cases  
- Installing **security cameras, mirrors, and warning signs**  
- Training employees to recognize suspicious behavior  

The rise in shoplifting is pushing retailers to reconsider how **brick-and-mortar stores operate and protect their merchandise**.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">

Water pollution arises from:
- Dumping toxic chemicals and raw sewage into rivers and oceans  
- Oil spills  
- Burial of industrial waste that seeps into underground water  
- Fertilizer and insecticide runoff from agriculture and landscaping

Air pollution is caused by:
- Smoke and chemical emissions from factories  
- Carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons from motor vehicles  

Consequences of air pollution include:
- Health risks to humans  
- Formation of **acid rain**, damaging forests and lakes  
- Contribution to **climate change** by trapping heat due to carbon dioxide accumulation  

Industrialization and increased vehicle ownership in countries like **China** and **India** accelerate climate change. Controlling carbon emissions, such as reducing peak electricity consumption, is critical to mitigating these effects.

### Land Pollution
Land pollution is closely linked to water pollution because chemicals dumped on land eventually reach water sources. Causes include:
- Residential and industrial waste  
- Strip mining  
- Forest fires and poor forest management  

Consequences:
- Contamination of waterways with prescription drugs, soaps, and other chemicals  
- Rising **CO2 levels**, with current atmospheric CO2 at highs not seen in 4 million years  
- Accelerated warming of the Arctic, contributing to climate change  

### Waste Disposal
Proper disposal of waste is essential to combat land pollution. Challenges include:
- **Plastic bags:** Americans use 100 billion per year; average family uses 1,500 annually  
- Plastic bags take **500+ years** to degrade in landfills  
- Many states and countries are phasing out lightweight plastic bags  

### Key Facts
- Global surface temperature is rising  
- Arctic warming occurs **twice as fast** as the rest of the world  
- Over **80% of people** believe CEOs should publicly address climate change  
- About **63 million tons of electronic waste** are generated annually, including laptops, phones, and TVs

<h1 id="722658" style="color:#E65100;">
  <a href="#Chapter_2._Business_Ethics_and_Social_Responsibility" style="color:inherit; text-decoration:none;">
    Sources of Law

  </a>
</h1>

### Definitions
- **Criminal Law** – Laws that prohibit specific actions (e.g., mail fraud, unfair competition) and impose punishment such as fines or imprisonment. Violations are called **crimes**.  
- **Civil Law** – Laws that define the rights and duties of individuals and organizations (including businesses). Violations may result in fines or other remedies but **not imprisonment**.  

**Key Difference:**  
- Criminal laws are enforced by the **state or nation**.  
- Civil laws are enforced through the **court system** by individuals or organizations.

### Four Primary Sources of Law
1. **Constitutional Law** – Laws derived from the **U.S. Constitution**, establishing the fundamental legal framework.  
2. **Common Law** – Laws created by **judicial precedents**, based on previous court rulings.  
3. **Statutory Law** – Laws enacted by **federal or state legislatures**.  
4. **Administrative Law** – Laws created and enforced by **federal and state administrative agencies** to regulate business practices, protect consumers, employees, and the environment.  

**Additional Notes:**  
- Federal administrative agencies established by **Congress** influence business by enforcing regulations.  
- The **Supreme Court** serves as the ultimate authority on legal and regulatory decisions regarding appropriate business conduct.


<h1 id="387287" style="color:#E65100;">
  <a href="#Chapter_2._Business_Ethics_and_Social_Responsibility" style="color:inherit; text-decoration:none;">
    Courts and the Resolution of Disputes
  </a>
</h1>

### Definitions
- **Lawsuit** – A legal action in which one individual or organization takes another to court to resolve a conflict, usually under **civil law**.  
- **Judgment** – A court's official decision in a lawsuit, which may require the losing party to pay **monetary damages** or take specific actions.  
- **Monetary Damages** – Money awarded by the court to compensate for harm or loss caused by the losing party.  
- **Specific Performance** – A court order requiring a party to **do or refrain from doing a particular act**.

### Key Points
- Courts provide a **forum** for resolving disputes based on criminal and civil laws.  
- Decisions are interpreted according to the **legal and regulatory system**.  
- Winning a lawsuit **does not guarantee collection** of monetary damages if the losing party lacks financial resources (e.g., bankruptcy).  
- Business lawsuits can seek **financial restitution** or **specific actions** to correct wrongdoing.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Court System
</span></h2>

### Definitions
- **Jurisdiction** – The legal authority of a court to **interpret and apply the law** and make a **binding decision** in a specific case.  
- **Court of General Jurisdiction** – A court authorized to hear **all types of cases**.  
- **Court of Limited Jurisdiction** – A court authorized to hear **only specific types of cases** (e.g., Federal Bankruptcy Court handles bankruptcy cases).  
- **Trial Court** – A court where the **facts of the case are determined** and the relevant **laws are applied** to resolve the dispute.  
- **Appellate Court** – A court that reviews the **interpretation of the law** in trial court decisions. It does **not hear witnesses** or determine facts.  
- **Appeal** – A request to have a higher court review a trial court’s decision for **legal errors**.  
- **New Trial / Remand** – When an appellate court sends a case back to the trial court to **correct mistakes** that could have affected the outcome.  

### Key Points
- Federal courts receive jurisdiction from the **Constitution or Congress**, while state courts are established by **state legislatures and constitutions**.  
- Trial courts resolve **facts and legal issues**; appellate courts review only **legal interpretations**.  
- If an appellate court finds no significant legal errors, the trial court’s decision **stands**.  
- Appellate courts can **remand the case** for correction or occasionally **modify the verdict** themselves.  

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) Methods
</span></h2>

### Definitions
- **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)** – Methods of resolving business disputes **outside of traditional court litigation** to save time and money.  
- **Mediation** – A voluntary process in which **one or more third-party mediators** help disputing parties **negotiate a settlement**. Mediators **suggest solutions**, but their recommendations are **nonbinding**.  
- **Arbitration** – A process where a **neutral arbitrator** hears the case and issues a **binding decision**. Arbitration may be **mandatory** if required by a contract.  
- **Mini-Trial** – A **summarized presentation** of a dispute to an independent advisor, who then provides guidance on the likely outcome, helping parties **negotiate a settlement** before going to court.  
- **Private Court** – A **nongovernmental court** where disputes are resolved by an independent third party; proceedings may be **informal or formal**, depending on the arrangement.  

### Key Points
- ADR methods are **faster and less expensive** than traditional lawsuits.  
- **Mediation** relies on voluntary agreement; **arbitration** results in a mandatory decision.  
- **Mini-trials** and **private courts** help businesses identify weaknesses in a case and encourage settlement **before full litigation**.  
- Parties can often **choose mediators or arbitrators** with expertise in the specific business area of the dispute.

### Definition
- **Regulatory Administrative Agency** – A federal or state agency empowered to **create, enforce, and interpret regulations** within a specific area of law. Some agencies also have **judicial powers** to resolve disputes involving their regulations.

### Key Points
- Agencies such as the **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)** can **decide disputes** under their regulatory authority.  
- The **resolution process** in these cases is typically called a **hearing**, rather than a trial.  
- An **Administrative Law Judge (ALJ)** presides over these hearings and **decides all issues** related to the dispute.  
- These agencies ensure compliance with laws and regulations in areas like **consumer protection, environmental law, labor, and finance**.

Due to overlapping responsibilities, confusion or conflict about which agency has jurisdiction over certain activities is common.

### The Federal Trade Commission (FTC)
- The FTC plays a major role in regulating business practices that could harm consumers or create disputes between businesses and their customers.

- **Enforcement tools:**
  - **Complaint:** Issued when a business is believed to be violating the law.
  - **Cease-and-desist order:** Commands the company to stop the objectionable practice.
  - **Civil penalties:** Up to $10,000 per day for violations.
  - **Consent decrees:** Address price-fixing or collusion attempts, even if competitors refuse invitations.
  - **Corrective advertising:** Requires companies to rectify misleading advertisements.

<h1 id="194515" style="color:#E65100;">
  <a href="#Chapter_2._Business_Ethics_and_Social_Responsibility" style="color:inherit; text-decoration:none;">
    Important Elements of Business Law
  </a>
</h1>

Business law provides the legal framework within which companies operate. Understanding these laws helps businesspeople **avoid violations of criminal and civil statutes** and **minimize the risk of lawsuits** from consumers, employees, suppliers, and other stakeholders. Key elements include:

- **Compliance with criminal laws:** Prevents actions such as fraud, theft, or unfair competition that could result in fines or imprisonment.
- **Adherence to civil laws:** Ensures that contracts, property rights, and business obligations are honored, reducing disputes and potential monetary damages.
- **Awareness of regulatory requirements:** Following rules set by federal and state agencies, including product safety, environmental standards, and labor regulations.
- **Proactive risk management:** Implementing internal policies, employee training, and ethics programs to prevent violations and promote lawful business conduct.

By integrating knowledge of these legal elements into daily operations, businesses can operate ethically, reduce legal exposure, and build trust with stakeholders.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)
</span></h2>

The **Uniform Commercial Code (UCC)** was created to standardize and simplify business transactions across states. Before its enactment, each state had its own laws governing commerce, making interstate business complex and inconsistent. Today, all states except Louisiana have fully adopted the UCC (Louisiana has adopted portions of it).

The UCC is a set of **statutory laws** covering various aspects of business law, including contracts, sales, and commercial paper. One of its most significant components is **Article II**, which governs the **sale of goods**. This article establishes uniform rules for contracts, obligations, and remedies related to buying and selling products, thereby providing legal consistency and reducing disputes in commerce.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Sales Agreements under Article II of the UCC
</span></h2>

**Scope:** Article II governs **sales agreements for goods**, including goods with services such as installation. It **does not** cover stocks, bonds, personal services, or real estate.

**Key Provisions:**
- A sales agreement can be enforced even if it **does not specify price, time, or place of delivery**.
- If price is not agreed upon, the buyer must pay a **reasonable price at delivery**.
- Addresses **rights of buyers and sellers**, **transfer of ownership**, **warranties**, and **allocation of risk** during manufacture and delivery.

**Warranties:**
- **Express Warranty:** Specific terms the seller promises to honor. Example: car manufacturers often provide a 3-year/36,000-mile warranty to repair specified defects.
- **Implied Warranty:** Automatically imposed by law even if not written. Includes assurances that the product:
  - Has a **clear title** (is not stolen)
  - Will **perform its intended purpose**
  - Will **function as advertised**


<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Law of Torts and Fraud
</span></h2>

## 1. Tort
- **Definition:** A private or civil wrong, other than a breach of contract, that causes harm or injury to another person or property.  
- **Example:** A Domino's Pizza delivery driver loses control of the vehicle, damaging property or injuring someone. Both the driver and the company can be sued.  
- **Importance:** Allows victims to seek compensation for damages caused by the negligent actions of others.

## 2. Fraud
- **Definition:** An intentional unlawful act to deceive or manipulate others to cause harm or gain a benefit. Fraud may be both a civil and criminal violation.  
- **Example:** Health care fraud, such as submitting false claims to receive improper payments from insurance companies.  
- **Importance:** Protects individuals and organizations from deliberate deception that could cause financial or personal harm.

## 3. Product Liability
- **Definition:** A business's legal responsibility for negligence in the design, production, sale, or use of its products.  
- **Example:** Defective products such as asbestos insulation, household pesticides, or lead paint on toys.  
- **Types:**
  - **Strict Product Liability:** The consumer only needs to prove the product was defective, the defect caused injury, and that the product was unreasonably dangerous. No need to prove manufacturer negligence.  
- **Importance:** Ensures manufacturers and sellers are accountable for the safety of their products.

## 4. Tort Reform
- **Definition:** Legal changes aimed at limiting large monetary awards in civil lawsuits, reducing the financial burden on businesses.  
- **Example:** Laws that cap compensation amounts in product liability or accident lawsuits.  
- **Importance:** Protects businesses from excessive judgments while maintaining consumer protection.

## 5. Context and Relevance
- Tort law, especially regarding product liability, is a major political and economic issue because multimillion-dollar lawsuits affect company finances and product prices.  
- While many lawsuits are justified, some are not; tort reforms aim to reduce abuse while still protecting consumers.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Law of Contracts
</span></h2> 

## Definition
A **contract** is a mutual agreement between two or more parties that can be enforced in a court if one party fails to comply with its terms. Contracts govern most business transactions, from leases to loans to sales agreements.

## Types of Contracts
- **Verbal/Handshake Contract:** A spoken agreement that is generally as binding as a written contract.  
- **Written Contract:** Required by law in certain cases, such as:
  - Sale of land or interest in land  
  - Promise to pay someone else’s debt  
  - Agreements that cannot be fulfilled within one year  
  - Sale of goods over $500 (Uniform Commercial Code requirement)  

## Elements of a Legal Contract
For a contract to be enforceable, it must include the following elements:

1. **Voluntary Agreement**  
   - An agreement freely and knowingly made by all parties.  
   - Must involve a valid **offer** and **acceptance**.  
   - Fraud, coercion, or force invalidates the agreement.

2. **Consideration**  
   - Something of value exchanged between parties.  
   - Can be money, goods, services, or promises.  
   - Without consideration, a promise is generally unenforceable.

3. **Contractual Capacity**  
   - The legal ability to enter a contract.  
   - Limited or absent for minors (under 18), mentally unstable, intellectually disabled, insane, or intoxicated individuals.

4. **Legality**  
   - The contract’s purpose and consideration must be lawful.  
   - Illegal contracts, such as usurious loans, are unenforceable.  
   - Performing an illegal act under an otherwise legal contract does not make the contract itself illegal.

## Breach of Contract
- Occurs when a party fails to fulfill their contractual obligations.  
- Example: Failing to pay rent on a leased apartment.  
- The breaching party may be liable for **monetary damages** to the other party.  

---

**Key Terms:**
- **Contract:** Legally enforceable agreement between parties.  
- **Voluntary Agreement:** Freely made offer and acceptance without fraud or coercion.  
- **Consideration:** Value exchanged in a contract.  
- **Contractual Capacity:** Legal ability to enter a contract.  
- **Legality:** Compliance with the law; contract must have a lawful purpose.  
- **Breach of Contract:** Failure to comply with contractual promises, potentially leading to damages.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Law of Agency
</span></h2>

## Definition
An **agency** is a business relationship in which one person (the **agent**) acts on behalf of another person (the **principal**) and under their control.

- **Principal:** The person who wishes to have a specific task accomplished.  
- **Agent:** The person who acts on behalf of the principal to accomplish the task.  

**Examples:** Authors, movie stars, and athletes often employ agents to negotiate contracts or secure opportunities.

## Creation of an Agency Relationship
- Typically created by **mutual agreement** between the principal and agent.  
- Not always required to be in writing, but **written agreements are advisable**.  
- Can be terminated by:
  - Mutual agreement  
  - Fulfillment of the agency's purpose  
  - Refusal of either party to continue  
  - Death of the principal or agent  

- **Power of Attorney:** A legal document granting authority to act as an agent. Common uses include:
  - Real estate transactions  
  - Financial transactions by accounting firms  
  - Legal matters (not limited to lawyers)

## Duties of Agents (Fiduciary Responsibilities)
Agents, especially corporate officers and directors, are considered **fiduciaries** — people entrusted with responsibilities requiring trust, care, and loyalty.

1. **Duty of Care (Duty of Diligence):**  
   - Make informed, careful, and thoughtful decisions.  
   - Agents are not held liable for negative outcomes if they act with due diligence.

2. **Duty of Loyalty:**  
   - All decisions must prioritize the interests of the principal and stakeholders.  
   - Conflicts of interest should be avoided.

**Example:**  
McDonald's added new board members with expertise in areas the company wanted to strengthen, such as global healthcare and franchising/hospitality.

---

**Key Terms:**
- **Agency:** A relationship where an agent acts on behalf of a principal.  
- **Principal:** The person requesting a task to be done.  
- **Agent:** The person performing the task on behalf of the principal.  
- **Power of Attorney:** Legal authorization to act as someone else's agent.  
- **Fiduciary:** Person trusted to act in the best interest of another.  
- **Duty of Care:** Obligation to make informed and careful decisions.  
- **Duty of Loyalty:** Obligation to act in the principal’s best interest.

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Law of Property
</span></h2>

## Definition
**Property law** governs the ownership and transfer of all types of property, including real, personal, and intellectual property.

### Types of Property
1. **Real Property**  
   - Land and anything permanently attached to it.  
   - Example: Buildings, homes, and fixtures.

2. **Personal Property**  
   - Everything else that is owned.  
   - **Tangible Property:** Physical items with existence.  
     - Examples: Automobiles, business inventory, clothing.  
   - **Intangible Property:** Rights and duties, often represented by a document.  
     - Examples: Accounts receivable, stock in a corporation, trademarks, goodwill.

3. **Intellectual Property (IP)**  
   - Creations generated by a person's creative activities.  
   - Examples: Musical works, books, artwork, computer software.

---

## Protection of Property

### Copyrights
- Protects ownership rights of intellectual property such as books, music, videos, photos, and software.  
- Duration: Lifetime of the creator + 50 years (usually).  
- Ensures exclusive rights to publish, reproduce, or distribute works.

### Patents
- Provide inventors exclusive rights to their inventions.  
- Duration: 20 years.  
- Particularly competitive in industries like pharmaceuticals.  
- Typically take 18 months to secure.  
- 3,000–4,000 patent lawsuits are filed annually in the U.S.

### Trademarks
- A brand name, symbol, or mark legally registered with the **U.S. Patent and Trademark Office**.  
- Protects against unauthorized use by other firms.  
- Examples: McDonald's golden arches.  
- **Trade Dress:** Visual appearance of a product or packaging.  
  - Example: Coca-Cola contoured bottle, Hershey’s chocolate bar panels.  
  - Protection is granted if consumers strongly associate the shape/design with the product.

---

## Challenges in Intellectual Property
- **Global Trade:** Securing protection abroad can be difficult.  
- Laws vary by country; some countries may not enforce U.S. IP laws.  
- Counterfeiting harms sales and reputation.  
- Examples:  
  - Pacific Mall, Markham, Ontario, Canada  
  - Silk Market, Beijing  
  - Open-air markets, Mexico City  

**Note:** Businesses trading internationally may need extra measures to protect IP rights due to insufficient local laws.

---

**Key Terms:**
- **Property Law:** Governs ownership and transfer of real, personal, and intellectual property.  
- **Real Property:** Land and permanent attachments.  
- **Personal Property:** Owned items other than real estate; tangible or intangible.  
- **Tangible Property:** Physical property with a real presence.  
- **Intangible Property:** Rights or duties represented in a document or symbol.  
- **Intellectual Property:** Creations of the mind, such as art, software, or inventions.  
- **Copyright:** Legal protection for creative works.  
- **Patent:** Legal protection for inventions.  
- **Trademark:** Legal protection for a brand name or symbol.  
- **Trade Dress:** Visual appearance of a product that identifies its source.  
- **Counterfeiting:** Unauthorized imitation of a product or brand.


<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
The Law of Bankruptcy
</span></h2>

## Definition
**Bankruptcy** is a legal process in which an individual or business unable to repay debts can seek relief from some or all financial obligations. It is sometimes called a "fresh start."

---

## Causes of Bankruptcy
1. **Individuals:**  
   - Charging goods and services beyond their ability to pay.
2. **Businesses:**  
   - Taking on excessive debt to finance growth.  
   - Experiencing unexpected business events such as commodity price increases or economic downturns.  
   - Examples of businesses declaring bankruptcy: Revlon, Hertz, JCPenney, Guitar Center, California Pizza Kitchen, Brooks Brothers, Cirque du Soleil (COVID-19 pandemic impact).

---

## Bankruptcy Process
- Individuals or companies petition a **bankruptcy court** to declare them unable to pay debts.  
- The debtor's **assets** may be sold to pay off creditors.  
- Personal bankruptcy provides a clean slate, but obtaining credit afterward is challenging.  
- Laws restrict who can file for bankruptcy to prevent abuse, making it harder for some consumers to clear their debts.  

---

## Types of Bankruptcy (Overview)
- **Chapter 7 Bankruptcy:**  
  - The most common form of personal bankruptcy.  
  - Allows individuals or companies to eliminate debts after liquidating assets.  
- **Other Types:**  
  - Vary based on the level of protection and whether the debtor reorganizes or liquidates.  
  - Both individuals and creditors can initiate bankruptcy proceedings.

---

## Key Terms
- **Bankruptcy:** Legal process for individuals or businesses unable to repay debts.  
- **Debtor:** Person or organization that owes money.  
- **Creditor:** Person or entity to whom money is owed.  
- **Liquidation:** Selling assets to repay debts.  
- **Fresh Start:** The principle allowing debtors relief from obligations to start anew.  
- **Chapter 7 Bankruptcy:** A form of bankruptcy that allows for debt discharge after asset liquidation.

| Trademark Law Revision Act (1988) | Amends the Lanham Act to allow brands not yet introduced to be protected through registration with the Patent and Trademark Office. |
| Nutrition Labeling and Education Act (1990) | Prohibits exaggerated health claims and requires all processed foods to include labels with nutritional information. |
| Telephone Consumer Protection Act (1991) | Establishes procedures to avoid unwanted telephone solicitations; prohibits use of automated dialing systems or prerecorded voices on certain lines. |
| Federal Trademark Dilution Act (1995) | Provides trademark owners the right to protect trademarks and requires relinquishment of names that conflict with existing trademarks. |

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Federal Laws Affecting Business Practices
</span></h2>

## Sherman Antitrust Act (1890)
**Definition:** Prevents businesses from restraining trade or monopolizing markets.  
**Purpose:** Condemns "every contract, combination, or conspiracy in restraint of trade."  
**Example:** A competitor agreement to fix prices or divide markets violates this act.  
**Notable Case:** Syngenta Crop Protection and Corteva, Inc. faced antitrust lawsuits for loyalty programs that limited distributors’ business with competitors.  
**Notes:** Still relevant today and serves as a model for fair competition laws worldwide.  

## Clayton Act (1914)
**Definition:** Limits specific business practices that reduce competition.  
**Purpose:** Prohibits price discrimination, tying and exclusive agreements, stock acquisitions that lessen competition, and dual board membership on competing companies.  
**Exemptions:** Farm cooperatives and labor organizations are exempt from antitrust restrictions.  
**Notes:** Designed to clarify and strengthen provisions of the Sherman Antitrust Act.  
**Practical Implications:** Helps determine acceptable competition levels and restricts monopolistic behavior, though interpretation may vary by judge or court.

## Background
**Cineworld Group**: One of the world's largest movie theater chains, based in London, operating:

**Financial Status at Filing**:  
- Debt: ~$9 billion (largely from COVID-19 pandemic lockdowns)  
- Cash on Hand: <$4 million  
- Financing Secured: ~$2 billion to support reorganization  

## Challenges Facing Movie Theaters
1. **COVID-19 Pandemic Impacts**: Lockdowns caused revenue loss; public hesitancy to attend theaters.  
2. **Competition from Streaming Services**: Netflix, Disney+, HBO Max offer at-home alternatives.  
3. **Delayed or Skipped Releases**: Studios released fewer blockbusters in theaters during the pandemic.  
4. **Underperforming Blockbusters**: Even major films are not drawing expected international audiences.  
5. **Changing Consumer Habits**: Preference for at-home entertainment, convenience, and flexible viewing options.  

## Strategies Cineworld Has Explored
- Membership packages

- Potential sale of business

## Critical Thinking Questions
1. **Challenges for Movie Theaters**: Pandemic-related closures, streaming competition, delayed releases, changing consumer behavior.

<h1 id="881528" style="color:#E65100;">
  <a href="#Chapter_2._Business_Ethics_and_Social_Responsibility" style="color:inherit; text-decoration:none;">
    The Internet: Legal and Regulatory Issues
  </a>
</h1>

## Overview
The growth of the internet creates **legal challenges for businesses**, primarily concerning:
- Consumer privacy  
- Proper competitive use of online data  
- Compliance with federal and state regulations  

Businesses must apply **traditional legal standards** (e.g., truth in advertising, contract law) to internet activities.  

---

## Key Concepts and Definitions

### Cookies
- **Definition**: Small pieces of data stored on a user’s device by a website to track visitor activity for recontact or analysis.  
- **Controversy**: Some see tracking as invasive; others see it as similar to traditional data collection by offline businesses.

### Personal Privacy Protection
- **Current Basis**: U.S. Constitution, Supreme Court rulings, and privacy laws such as:  
  - **Fair Credit Reporting Act (1971)**  
  - **Right to Financial Privacy Act (1978)**  
  - **Privacy Act (1974)**  

- **State-Level Example**:  
  - **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)**  
  - **California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA)**  
  - **Purpose**: Gives consumers control over data, limits retention, and protects security.  

- **Comparison**: The U.S. currently lacks a comprehensive federal privacy law like the EU's **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**.

### Children's Online Privacy
- **Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA, 2000)**:  
  - Prohibits websites and online services from collecting personal information from children under 13 without parental consent.  
  - **Example Case**: Google and YouTube paid $170 million for violating COPPA by collecting children's data without consent.  

### FTC Rules for Online Advertising
- Online advertising follows **the same rules as traditional advertising**:
  - Must be truthful  
  - Cannot mislead consumers  
  - Claims must be substantiated  

- **Influencer Marketing Requirements**:
  - Disclose all material connections with brands (paid or free products)  
  - Cases filed against Teami Blends, Lord & Taylor, Warner Bros. Home Entertainment, Machinima, ADT  

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Businesses must **respect consumer privacy** and comply with privacy laws at both federal and state levels.  
2. **Children’s data require special protection** under COPPA.  
3. Online advertising must **maintain transparency** and truthfulness.  
4. **Influencers** are legally responsible to disclose sponsorships or gifts to avoid deceptive advertising claims.  

<h1 id="157036" style="color:#E65100;">
  <a href="#Chapter_2._Business_Ethics_and_Social_Responsibility" style="color:inherit; text-decoration:none;">
    Legal Pressure for Responsible Business Conduct
  </a>
</h1>

## Overview
Governments at both federal and state levels are increasingly holding **organizations accountable** for misconduct, not just individual employees.  

Before 1991:  
- Laws primarily punished the employees directly responsible for illegal acts.  

After the Federal Sentencing Guidelines for Organizations (FSGO):  
- Both the **responsible employees** and the **employing firms** can be held accountable for violations of federal law.  
- Responsibility for **controlling and preventing misconduct** falls on **top management**.  

---

## Federal Sentencing Guidelines for Organizations (FSGO)

### Objectives
1. Train employees  
2. Self-monitor and supervise employee conduct  
3. Deter unethical acts  
4. Punish organizational members who engage in illegal acts  

### 2010 Amendment Highlights
- Ethics officers report directly to the **board of directors**, not just general counsel or top officers.  
- The **board of directors** must:
  - Assign a high-ranking manager accountable for daily oversight of ethics programs  
  - Provide authority, resources, and access to the board or its subcommittees  
  - Maintain **confidential reporting mechanisms** for employees to report misconduct without retaliation  
  - Oversee risk discovery and implement approaches to manage those risks  

---

## Importance of Organizational Ethics
- Organizations that **fail to comply** with FSGO minimum requirements may face:
  - Legal penalties  
  - Loss of customer trust and public confidence  
  - Loss of intangible assets  

- A **legalistic approach alone is insufficient**. Organizations must cultivate **high ethical standards** to prevent illegal conduct.  

- Ethics and compliance have become top priorities for corporate lawyers due to:
  - Sarbanes-Oxley Act  
  - Dodd-Frank Act  
  - Federal Sentencing Guidelines  

---

## Key Definitions

- **Fiduciary Responsibility**: The duty of top management to act in the best interests of the organization, ensuring ethical and lawful operations.  
- **Ethics Officer**: A manager responsible for overseeing compliance with ethical standards and reporting misconduct directly to the board.  
- **Compliance Program**: Organizational policies and procedures designed to ensure adherence to laws, regulations, and ethical standards.  
- **Confidential Reporting Mechanism**: A system that allows employees to report unethical or illegal conduct anonymously without fear of retaliation.  

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Seven Steps to Compliance (FSGO) – Table A.5
</span></h2>

To ensure organizational accountability and reduce misconduct, the **Federal Sentencing Guidelines for Organizations (FSGO)** outline seven key steps:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Develop standards and procedures to reduce the propensity for criminal conduct. |
| 2 | Designate a high-level compliance manager or ethics officer to oversee the compliance program. |
| 3 | Avoid delegating authority to people known to have a propensity to engage in misconduct. |
| 4 | Communicate standards and procedures to employees, agents, and contractors through training and publications. |
| 5 | Establish systems to monitor and audit misconduct, including reporting mechanisms for employees and agents. |
| 6 | Enforce standards and punishments consistently across all employees in the organization. |
| 7 | Respond immediately to misconduct and take reasonable steps to prevent further criminal conduct. |


## Compliance Programs

Federal guidelines require businesses to develop programs that **detect and deter employee misconduct**. For a compliance program to be effective, it must include:

- **Disclosure of wrongdoing**: Employees and agents can report illegal or unethical behavior.  
- **Cooperation with government authorities**: Organizations must assist in investigations or enforcement actions.  
- **Acceptance of responsibility**: The organization acknowledges misconduct and takes corrective measures.

### Typical Components of Compliance Programs

- **Codes of ethics**: Written standards for ethical and legal behavior.  
- **Employee ethics training**: Regular education on ethical conduct and compliance requirements.  
- **Hotlines**: Confidential reporting channels, e.g., direct 800 phone numbers.  
- **Compliance directors**: High-level managers overseeing adherence to legal and ethical standards.  
- **Communication tools**: Newsletters, brochures, and other methods to reinforce compliance.  

**Purpose:** The ethics component acts as a **buffer**, helping firms avoid crossing the line between unethical and illegal conduct.

---

## Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX) Overview

- **Passed:** Early 2000s, in response to corporate ethics scandals.  
- **Purpose:** Criminalizes securities fraud and strengthens penalties for corporate fraud.  
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Creation of an **accounting oversight board**.
  - Requires corporations to establish **codes of ethics for financial reporting**.  
  - Promotes **greater transparency** in financial statements to investors and other stakeholders.  
  - Top executives must **sign off on financial reports** and face fines or jail if the reports are false.  

**Significance:** SOX increases accountability and reinforces compliance and ethical conduct at the highest levels of corporate leadership.

</span></h2>

| No. | Provision | Description |
|-----|-----------|-------------|

<h2><span style="color: #ffbb00; padding:2px;">
Corporate Regulatory Laws: Sarbanes-Oxley and Dodd-Frank
</span></h2>

## Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)
- **Purpose:** Improve corporate governance, accountability, and transparency in financial reporting.
- **Concerns:**
  - Considered burdensome and expensive for corporations.
  - Large corporations may spend over $2 million per year to comply.
  - Did not prevent financial misconduct leading to the 2008 Great Recession.
- **Benefits:**
  - Greater accountability of top managers and boards of directors.
  - Improves investor confidence.
  - Protects employees, especially retirement plans.
  - Better-informed boards can lead to improved efficiency and cost savings (e.g., Cisco, Pitney Bowes).

## Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010)
- **Purpose:** Reform Wall Street and the financial industry following the 2008 financial crisis.
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Increased consumer protection.
  - Enhanced transparency and accountability in the financial sector.
  - Created new financial agencies:
    1. **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)** – protects consumers from complex or deceptive financial products.
    2. **Office of Financial Research** – monitors financial stability.
    3. **Financial Stability Oversight Council** – works to prevent future systemic crises.
- **Whistleblower Protections:**
  - Offers financial incentives for reporting misconduct.
  - If a reported violation results in penalties over $1 million, the whistleblower receives a percentage of the settlement.
- **Financial Transparency:**
  - Complex financial instruments must be disclosed more clearly to consumers.
- **Relationship to SOX:**
  - Builds on Sarbanes-Oxley provisions.
  - Strengthens protections and accountability beyond SOX.
- **Current Impact:** Still evolving; too early to fully assess long-term effectiveness but already leading to significant regulatory changes on Wall Street.
